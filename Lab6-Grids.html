Introduction

In this lab, we explore the visual evolution of a two-dimensional complex grid under iterative transformations of the form \( z_{i+1} = z_i^n - c \), where \( z \in \mathbb{C} \) and \( c \) is a constant (in this case, 0.5). The grid is initialized over a square region of the complex plane ranging from -1 to 1 in both real and imaginary axes. Through iterative application of the transformation, we analyze how the values evolve with varying powers \( n \) and iteration counts, visually represented through dynamic plotting of the real and imaginary parts. This approach offers a computational perspective on fractal formation and complex dynamical systems. An advantage of using Wolfram Mathematica lies in its symbolic engine and dynamic interactivity—particularly through functions like Manipulate, which allow seamless adjustment of parameters in real time, making it exceptionally suited for mathematical experimentation and visualization. Furthermore, Mathematica handles complex arithmetic and large data visualizations efficiently, providing an immediate and visually rich representation of results. As Stephen Wolfram (2002) highlights in A New Kind of Science, simple iterative processes can generate unexpectedly intricate behavior, a principle clearly observable in this lab’s fractal-based experiment (Wolfram, 2002).

1. Animating with Wolfram Mathematica

The Python script below visualizes the Cantor Set through recursive geometric construction and plotting. It begins by importing NumPy and Matplotlib to handle numerical operations and graphical rendering. Initial parameters define the horizontal span, vertical positioning, bar height, spacing between levels, and recursion depth. The cantor_set function implements the fractal logic: at each level, it divides a line segment into three equal parts, removes the middle third, and recursively applies the same operation to the remaining left and right thirds, shifting them downward and reducing the recursion depth until reaching the base case. The output, a list of segments, is converted into a NumPy array for efficient plotting. Each segment is drawn as a black horizontal bar using fill_between, and the result is a stacked, self-similar structure representing the Cantor Set. The axes are hidden to emphasize the fractal pattern, and the plot is displayed in a clean format. 


cloudObj = 
 CloudDeploy[
  Manipulate[
  Module[{size = 50, x, y, grid, z}, x = Subdivide[-1, 1, size - 1];
  y = Subdivide[-1, 1, size - 1];
  grid = Flatten[Outer[Complex, x, y], 1];
  z = grid;
  Do[z = z^2 - 0.5, {i, 1, iterations}];
  ListPlot[ReIm /@ z, PlotStyle -> Blue, AspectRatio -> 1, 
  PlotRange -> All, AxesLabel -> {"Re", "Im"}, 
  PlotLabel -> 
  Row[{"Fractal Grid after ", iterations, " iteration(s)"}], 
  ImageSize -> Large]], {{iterations, 1, "Iterations"}, 0, 10, 1}]]

The image below depicts the original Cantor Set, constructed by recursively removing the middle third of a horizontal segment at each level, resulting in a self-similar fractal composed of increasingly fragmented line segments. This geometric process visually embodies the foundational ideas of set theory and early 20th-century explorations into the nature of the infinite and discontinuous (Dauben, 1990).

By modifying the number of preserved segments or introducing randomness, each version highlights different dimensions of complexity, symmetry, and visual rhythm within recursive systems. As recursion progresses, the figure takes on a dense, columnar appearance reminiscent of tridents or stylized organ pipes, highlighting the fractal’s increasing intricacy.

Conclusion

Through the original Cantor Set and its two variations, this lab demonstrates how recursive rules can generate rich and diverse fractal structures from simple principles. By adjusting the logic of segment removal—either deterministically or randomly—we observe shifts in symmetry, density, and visual rhythm. These explorations reveal the expressive potential of recursion in both mathematical theory and creative design.
